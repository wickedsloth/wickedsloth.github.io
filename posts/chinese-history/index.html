(Snippets) of Chinese History

Tue 06 March 2018

Modern Era: Late Qing Dynasty and birth of the Republic of China.

First Opium War (1841)

After its military victories, the British Empire forced China to concede treaty ports like Shanghai and Guangzhou.

Taiping Rebellion (1850-1864)

Hong Xiuquan, a Chinese student born third and youngest son of a poor Hakka family, met with Edwin Stevens, a foreign missionary in Guangzhou. Failing the imperial examination for three times led to a nervous breakdown. During his convalescence, Hong dreamt of visiting the heaven and viewed himself as the literal son of God and younger brother of Jesus Christ.

Consequently, Hong started to preach his visions and divine mission to his community and destroyed Confucian and Buddhist statues. The prophet Isaiah was of particular influence on Hong.

Your country is desolate, your cities burned with fire; your fields are being stripped by foreigners right before you, laid waste as when overthrown by strangers.

Isaiah 1:7

The mountains of Guanxi were fertile ground for rebellion. By 1850, Hong had between 10,000 to 30,000 followers.

End of the Qing Dynasty

The Qing Dynasty, exhausted after years of foreign invasion, the Taiping and Boxer Rebellions (1899-1901), collapsed with the death of Emperor Guangxu, likely poisoned, and the Empress Dowager Cixi in 1908. The succession goes to the two year old nephew to the deceased Emperor with Zaifeng as regent. However, the created cabinet, known as the "Royal Cabinet" was viewed negatively.

On October 10, 1911, the Wuchang Uprizing led to the creation of the Republic of China in Nanjing with Sun Yat-sen as its provisional head.
